# Branding Guidelines

## Foundation

Our mission is to make unlimited analytical insight available for every student. We achieve this by helping them understand exactly where their learning breaks down. This document defines how we communicate and express that mission through design, voice, and visual identity.

**See [`MISSION.md`](./MISSION.md) for our core values and operating principles.**

## How Our Values Translate to Brand

**Our core values (Trust, Excellence, Intelligence) are defined in [MISSION.md](./MISSION.md).**

Here's how each value guides our design and communication:

### Trust → Dependability

**What it means for our brand:**
- Consistent, predictable interfaces (no surprises, no hidden features)
- Reliable performance (fast, stable, always available)
- Transparent communication (clear about capabilities and limitations)
- Honest error messages (explain what went wrong, what to do next)

**Visual expression:**
- Stable, consistent layouts and patterns
- Professional, polished appearance
- Clear visual hierarchy (always know what to focus on)
- Reliable color coding (same colors = same meanings)

**Voice expression:**
- Make clear promises and deliver on them
- Admit limitations honestly ("We can't mark diagrams yet")
- Explain what happened when things go wrong
- Never overpromise or use marketing hyperbole

### Excellence → Quality

**What it means for our brand:**
- High-quality design at every touchpoint
- Attention to detail (typography, spacing, alignment)
- Zero tolerance for sloppiness (no typos, no broken layouts)
- Premium feel that reflects serious exam preparation

**Visual expression:**
- Polished, refined interfaces (generous spacing, thoughtful hierarchy)
- Premium typography (readable, professional, appropriate to context)
- Precise alignment and spacing (nothing feels haphazard)
- Smooth interactions and transitions (no jarring movements)

**Voice expression:**
- Grammatically correct, well-structured sentences
- Precise language (specific, not vague)
- No casual slang or internet speak
- Professional but not stuffy

### Intelligence → Insight

**What it means for our brand:**
- Sophisticated layouts that handle complex information
- Data-rich displays without overwhelming users
- Clear information hierarchy (progressive disclosure)
- Smart defaults and helpful suggestions

**Visual expression:**
- Sophisticated use of space and hierarchy
- Clear presentation of complex marking feedback
- LaTeX rendering that's crisp and readable
- Visual patterns that reveal structure (grouping, alignment, color)

**Voice expression:**
- Analytical, specific feedback ("Step 3: sign error when expanding brackets")
- Pattern recognition ("You consistently make this type of error")
- Actionable recommendations ("Practice questions involving negative coefficients")
- Explanations, not just judgments

## UX Principles

These principles guide how we implement our operating principles (from MISSION.md) in practice:

### 1. Calm

Students are preparing for high-stakes exams. Our interface should reduce stress, not amplify it. Every interaction should feel peaceful, unhurried, and supportive.

**This means:**
- Soft, muted color palette (no harsh reds, no bright neons)
- Generous spacing (breathing room, not cramped)
- Smooth, gentle transitions (no jarring animations)
- Neutral feedback colors (amber for incorrect, not harsh red)
- No time pressure or urgency language ("Try again" not "Fix this now!")

### 2. Approachable

Professional tools don't need to be intimidating. Our interface should feel accessible and supportive without sacrificing analytical precision or excellence. We're serious about exam preparation, but we meet students where they are.

**This means:**
- Modern visual language that students recognize and respect
- Professional without being corporate or cold
- Supportive tone without being patronizing
- Color choices that reduce stress, not amplify it
- Interface feels like a capable tool, not an authority figure

### 3. Clear

Students need to understand their mistakes immediately. Clarity is non-negotiable. If feedback is confusing, it's useless.

**This means:**
- Simple navigation (always know where you are, how to get back)
- Clear visual hierarchy (most important = most prominent)
- Plain language (no jargon, no acronyms without explanation)
- One clear next step (never ambiguous about what to do next)
- LaTeX rendering optimized for readability (font size, contrast, spacing)

### 4. Effortless

Friction kills retention. Every interaction should feel easy, fast, and obvious. Students shouldn't have to think about the interface - it should disappear.

**This means:**
- Minimal taps to complete tasks (photo → mark → result in 3 taps)
- Smart defaults (pre-select most common options)
- No unnecessary forms or input fields
- Instant feedback (no loading spinners unless absolutely necessary)
- Obvious affordances (buttons look like buttons, tappable things look tappable)

### 5. Encouraging

Mistakes are part of learning. Exam prep is hard. Our tone should motivate students and celebrate progress, not judge or criticize.

**This means:**
- Progress-focused language ("You improved by 2 marks!")
- Positive framing ("Almost there! Let's look at step 3")
- Celebrate effort ("You've practiced 15 questions this week")
- No harsh judgment ("Incorrect" → "Let's take another look")
- Growth mindset messaging (mistakes = learning opportunities)

### 6. On-Demand

Students initiate all interactions. We respond when called upon, but never intrude uninvited. The interface should feel available but not pushy.

**This means:**
- No unsolicited notifications (unless explicitly opted in)
- No guilt-tripping about inactivity
- No engagement gamification (streaks, leaderboards, social pressure)
- Clear way to pause or exit any flow
- Interface disappears when not needed (no persistent badges or alerts)

## Relationship Stance

We are a tool in the student's arsenal, not their coach or teacher. Students are in control - they decide when and how to use us. We exist to serve their goals, on their terms.

**This affects design by:**
- Never forcing actions (everything is optional)
- Never prescribing behavior (suggest, don't command)
- Never making students feel guilty (no "You should..." language)
- Always letting students exit flows (no trapped states)
- Respecting student autonomy (they know what they need)

**Voice implications:**
- Use suggestions, not commands: "Consider practicing..." not "You must practice..."
- Offer information, don't prescribe: "Students who practice X improve by 15%" not "Practice X now"
- Celebrate student decisions: "You chose to focus on..." not "We assigned you..."
- Frame as partnership: "Let's look at..." not "Here's what you did wrong"

## Brand Voice

### Tone Attributes

**Respectful:** We recognize students are capable and motivated. We don't talk down to them or patronize them. We believe they can achieve their goals and treat them as intelligent partners in their learning.

**Supportive:** We're on their side. We want them to succeed. We provide help, not judgment. Our feedback is encouraging and focuses on growth, celebrating progress and framing mistakes as learning opportunities.

**Precise:** We're specific and clear. No vague feedback or hand-waving. Exactness shows respect for their intelligence. We identify exact failure points and provide actionable next steps.

### Voice Guidelines

**DO:**
- Use "Let's" language (collaborative) - "Let's look at question 3"
- Be specific about mistakes - "Step 2: sign error when expanding $(x-3)^2$"
- Celebrate improvement - "You improved by 2 marks since last attempt!"
- Frame mistakes as learning - "Common mistake: forgetting to simplify fractions"
- Use plain language - "Your answer" not "Candidate response"

**DON'T:**
- Use imperatives - "Fix this!" "Practice now!"
- Be vague - "Incorrect approach" (say what specifically went wrong)
- Be judgmental - "You failed to..." (use neutral language)
- Use exam-speak - "AO1", "SPaG", "QWC" (unless explaining mark schemes)
- Create urgency - "Only 3 days until exam!" "Practice now or fall behind!"

### Example Voice Applications

| Situation | ❌ Bad | ✅ Good |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **Incorrect answer** | "Wrong. Try again." | "Let's take another look at step 3" |
| **Partial credit** | "Partially correct" | "You got the method right, but there's a small error in step 2" |
| **Upload error** | "Error: Invalid image" | "Hmm, we couldn't read that image. Mind taking another photo?" |
| **No recent activity** | "You haven't practiced in 5 days!" | (No message - respect student autonomy) |

## Visual Direction

### Color Philosophy

**Goal:** Calm, trustworthy, premium feel. Colors should support content, not distract from it.

**What colors should represent:**
- **Trust and reliability** - Professional, dependable (not corporate cold)
- **Calm focus** - Reduces stress, supports concentration
- **Excellence** - Premium feel without being flashy
- **Encouragement** - Warm and supportive for positive feedback
- **Clarity** - Neutral tones that don't compete with content

**Feedback color meanings:**
- **Correct** - Positive reinforcement without celebration excess
- **Incorrect** - Attention needed without harsh judgment (avoid anxiety-inducing tones)
- **Partial credit** - Neutral, informational
- **Neutral elements** - Backgrounds, secondary text, UI chrome

**Colors to avoid:**
- Harsh, anxiety-inducing tones (red for errors)
- Bright, distracting neons
- Overly saturated colors

**Functional requirements:**
- High contrast for text (readable in bright daylight)
- Color-blind friendly (don't rely on color alone for critical information)
- Works on both light and dark backgrounds

### Typography Approach

**Goal:** Readable, professional, friendly (not playful or corporate)

**Characteristics:**
- Highly readable at mobile sizes
- Professional but not stiff or corporate
- Clear distinction between regular, bold, and headings
- Excellent LaTeX rendering (math must be crystal clear)

**Hierarchy:**
- Clear size/weight differences between levels
- Generous line height (especially for math/LaTeX)
- Adequate spacing between sections

### Layout Principles

**Goal:** Spacious, focused, clear hierarchy

**Principles:**
- **Generous spacing** - Content breathes, never cramped
- **Clear focus** - One primary action per screen
- **Visual hierarchy** - Most important = largest/boldest
- **Consistent patterns** - Same layouts for similar tasks
- **Progressive disclosure** - Show essential info first, details on demand

**Mobile-first considerations:**
- Thumb-friendly tap targets (minimum 44x44pt)
- One-handed operation where possible
- Important actions in easy-to-reach zones
- Minimal scrolling for critical tasks

### Iconography

**Icon Library:** [Lucide Icons](https://lucide.dev) (exclusively)

**Why Lucide:**
- **Consistency** - Strict design rules ensure uniform stroke width and style across 1,600+ icons
- **Scalability** - Comprehensive library supports multi-subject expansion (maths, chemistry, biology, physics)
- **Open Source** - MIT license, community-maintained fork of Feather Icons
- **Outline-first** - Clean, minimal outline style aligns with our Calm and Professional principles

**Usage Guidelines:**
- Use Lucide icons exclusively (no mixing with other icon sets)
- Prefer outline style for consistency with calm, professional aesthetic
- Standard sizes follow clear visual hierarchy:
  - **26px** - Secondary actions (header utilities, navigation)
  - **28px** - Tertiary actions (inline, contextual)
  - **38px** - Primary CTAs (add buttons, main actions)
- Icons should always have consistent stroke weight within the same context
- Size differential reinforces hierarchy: Primary actions are ~46% larger than secondary
